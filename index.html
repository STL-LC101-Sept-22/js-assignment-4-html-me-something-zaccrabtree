A life-changing trip to Moscow

An Unforgettable Trip


In 2015, I was a married father of three whose main hobby, other than home schooling, was singing in my acapella Russian Church choir. I had a love for all things Russian and musical, but my economic and family situation seemed to rule out any sort of excursion there for the foreseeable future.

That fall, however, I learned of a Russian choral institute for Russian-American church singers called the Patriarch Tikhon Russian-American Music Institute (PATRAM), which held a masterclass in Moscow every year for church musicians. I was simultaneously elated and dejected: it seemed like an impossible dream, but at the same time I felt an impulse to explore the class as a possibility.

In my research of the class, I was surprised to discover that they were awarding scholarships for the class tuition if one applied and had endorsements from parish clergy. One local priest wrote a recommendation letter for me, and not only did PATRAM give me full tuition, but they also paid for my airfare and my lodging at a local hotel!

Before I knew it, I was in Moscow with my good friend, who had also applied for a scholarship. Eight hours a day for an entire week, we practiced choral music, culminating being able to sing an entire vigil service on Saturday evening and a divine liturgy on Sunday morning with members of the Moscow State Conservatory, in one of the ancient churches of Russia's capital.

Between all our classwork and rehearsals, we toured Moscow on foot, by cab, and via the extensive subway system. The city itself was beautiful, fascinating, and vibrant. There were so many different types of people, so many cultural monuments, and so many beautiful museums! One one block was a huge WWII museum. Down the street was a privately owned museum with the largest collection of Ethiopian religious imagery in the world, outside of the African nation itself!

The eight days in Moscow passed in a whirlwind, but the memories of the trip, the lessons from the music class, the experience of the music itself, and the friends I made, have all remained with me to this day.

In fact, I answered interview questions for an Orthodox website about my trip. I think perhaps my response to one question in particular sums up the singing experience of my trip:
The goals, as I understood them, were to train a group of American musicians in the several pieces chosen, so that we actually sounded not only like a professional choir, but like a professional Russian Orthodox choir. I am in no position myself to judge this, but Maestro Gorbik told us afterwards that the monastery was very pleased with us. Others of the faithful in attendance also expressed their great appreciation. Maestro Gorbik himself also congratulated us on a job well done. This was enough for me.
